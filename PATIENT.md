## Who he is

My grandfather is **90 years old**. He lives in **his own home**, but the family helps him a lot —
with shopping, cleaning, and getting to appointments. Staying in his own home, rather than moving
into care, matters enormously to him. He reads and speaks **English**, so he would use the toolkit
in English.

## His back

He has **long-term low back pain** — it has been with him for a long time *(confirm roughly how long,
and what first set it off)*. At 90 it sits alongside the normal stiffness and slowing-down of age.
It is the kind of persistent, mechanical back pain the toolkit is built for — **provided a doctor or
physiotherapist has checked it first** (see the clinical note below; at his age this matters more,
not less).

## The real problem: he holds back from moving, and he is afraid of falling

This is the heart of his case. Because of the pain — and the fear of falling — **he avoids moving**.
He sits for long stretches, gets up slowly and carefully, and does less and less. The result is the
cycle the toolkit is designed to break:

> **pain + fear of falling → less movement → weaker legs and worse balance → higher fall risk and
> more pain → even less movement**

The less he moves, the weaker and less steady he becomes, which makes both the pain and the fall
risk worse — and brings closer the day he can no longer manage at home.

## What is at stake for him

For a man of 90 this is not abstract. Every bit of strength and confidence he holds onto is a bit
longer **living in his own home**, doing things for himself, staying part of family life. That is
his quality of life, and it is exactly what the toolkit's "keep moving, stay independent" idea means
for him.

## Why this toolkit suits him

| His situation | How the toolkit helps |

| Afraid that moving will harm his back | The "Hurt isn't harm" and "Less fear, more life" cards address the fear directly |

## Clinical note (read before he uses it)

At 90, two things matter more than for a younger patient:

- **Red flags must be cleared by a clinician first.** Older bones fracture more easily, so new or
  changed back pain deserves a proper check. The app's red **"When to get checked"** screen lists
  the warning signs, but it is a backup, not a substitute — confirm a GP or physiotherapist has
  assessed him and is happy for him to do gentle self-management *(confirm)*.
- **Choose the safest exercises for him.** Getting down to the floor may not be wise. Favour the
  pelvic tilt and knee-to-chest done **on a bed**, very short walks **with support**, and skip
  anything that feels unsafe — ideally checked with his physiotherapist.

His picture — fear of movement and falling, with deconditioning — is exactly what geriatric
physiotherapy and the guidelines say to treat with **reassurance, gentle graded activity, and
rebuilding confidence**, not rest. That is what the toolkit delivers.

## His goal

What does *he* most want to keep being able to do? Ask him, and write it into the app. For a man of
90 it might be something like *(confirm with him)*:

> "Get to the kitchen and the bathroom on my own," or "potter in the garden again," or simply
> "stay in my own home."

## How I would trial it with him

1. **Sit with him** and set it up together — magnets on his fridge, the app in English if he wants
   it — and agree his goal.
2. **Talk through the fear** using "Less fear, more life" and "Hurt isn't harm," then try one or two
   of the safe, gentle exercises together so he feels secure.
3. **Over a few weeks**, the family helps him do a little, often; the magnets are the daily nudge.
4. **Check back in** and look at what changed — is he moving a bit more, feeling steadier, more
   confident? — and note it for the assignment.

## What to measure (to show whether it helped)

- **Movement:** is he doing the gentle exercises, and moving around the house a little more?
- **Confidence / fear:** before vs after — "how worried are you that moving will hurt your back?"
  and "how afraid are you of falling?" on a simple 0–10 scale.
- **His goal:** is he closer to it (steadier on his feet, getting to the kitchen more easily)?
- **Which format he used:** the app, the magnets, or both — useful for the LO2 "evaluate two or
  more formats" point.
